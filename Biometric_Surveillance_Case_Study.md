# Biometric Surveillance: When Algorithms Decide Who We Are

### **1. Application Summary: The Pervasive Eye**
The case of Nijeer Parks serves as a chilling reminder of what happens when we place blind faith in technology. In early 2019, Parks became the third person known to be arrested for a crime he simply did not commit, all because of a flawed facial recognition match (Hill 2020). Parks was falsely accused of shoplifting and attempting to hit a police officer with his car; yet, while these events were unfolding, he was actually thirty miles away. This incident isn't just a "glitch"—it’s a systemic failure. As noted by Balli (2021), facial recognition technology is notoriously biased: it works well for white men, but its accuracy plummets for Black women and even white women. The real danger arises when the police trust the software more than the individual standing in front of them.

Biometric surveillance, at its core, is the use of our most personal data—our physical features—to track and observe us. While human observation has existed for centuries, AI has fundamentally changed the scale. In the past, a single observer could only watch one or two people at a time. Today, machine learning and high-definition CCTV allow for "community surveillance," where entire populations are tracked simultaneously. This expansion is why biometric surveillance is at the heart of modern privacy concerns. Academic research, such as the actionable audit by **Raji and Buolamwini (2019)** presented at the **AAAI/ACM Conference on AI, Ethics, and Society (AIES)**, has shown that commercial AI is often deployed into society without being properly tested for these life-altering biases.

### **2. Ethical Implications: Shattering the "Protective Cocoon"**
There is something deeply unsettling about being watched without your knowledge. This pervasive surveillance does more than just lead to false arrests; it damages our psyche. Humans have a fundamental need for a "protective cocoon"—a private space away from external scrutiny (Brown 2000). This cocoon is essential for what sociologists call "ontological security," a basic requirement for mental health and stability.

When we know we are being watched by an invisible algorithmic eye, we begin to self-censor. This is known as "social cooling"—a subtle modification of our behavior caused by the fear of being flagged or sanctioned (Schep n.d.). If we are afraid to attend a protest, speak freely in a park, or even walk through a certain neighborhood because of biometric tracking, we lose a part of our humanity. The technical side of this is equally problematic. The landmark "Gender Shades" study by **Buolamwini and Gebru (2018)** (presented at **ACM FAccT**) proved that these systems are built on datasets that favor specific demographics, leading to the "misidentification machine" that caught Nijeer Parks in its gears.

### **3. Global Future Impact: A Chilling Reality**
If we continue to roll out biometric surveillance globally, we risk a future where our innocence is no longer presumed, but must be proved against an algorithm's verdict. The long-term societal impact would be a permanent state of performance, where citizens act the way they believe the AI wants them to act. 

As discussed in the **ACM FAccT 2023** paper, **"A Sociotechnical Audit: Assessing Police Use of Facial Recognition" (Abdu et al.)**, these technologies reshape the relationship between the state and its citizens. We are moving toward a world where the "presumption of innocence" is replaced by "technological determinism." My opinion is that if we do not implement a global moratorium—or at least radically transparent standards—biometric AI will become a tool for systemic oppression rather than security. We must ask ourselves: is the convenience of automated tracking worth the loss of our ontological security and the freedom to exist without being a data point?
